Game of Life

John Conway

In 1970, British mathematician John Horton Conway introduced the world to what would become one of the most famous examples of cellular automata: The Game of Life. What began as a simple experiment in computation and mathematics quickly grew into a symbol of how complexity can emerge from simplicity.

Conway was fascinated by the idea of simulating life using a basic system. In the Game of Life, the "universe" consists of a two-dimensional grid of cells. Each cell can be either "alive" or "dead," and its fate is determined by its neighboring cells. Despite its simplicity, the game produces incredibly complex and beautiful patterns, evolving over time to create gliders, oscillators, and other fascinating structures.

The Game of Life is more than just a game—it’s a mathematical model that demonstrates how simple processes can lead to unpredictable and intricate behaviors, much like life itself.

About John Conway

John Horton Conway was a brilliant and eccentric mathematician, known for his groundbreaking work in several fields including group theory, number theory, and geometry. His playful nature and love for puzzles were well-known, and the Game of Life was born out of this curiosity. He wanted to create a system that could model the unpredictable nature of life in the simplest way possible, using only basic rules and a grid of cells. Though he created many mathematical marvels throughout his career, the Game of Life remains one of his most popular legacies, inspiring scientists, mathematicians, and curious minds alike. Conway passed away in 2020, but his work continues to live on in the countless variations of Life that still fascinate people today.
